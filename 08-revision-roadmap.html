8  A minimal-revision roadmap (and the questions to expect)

The dissertation is done, and the committee approved it. What follows is not a verdict on that work — it is a runway for what comes next. The central arguments about MFRM and HRM sensitivity and robustness are worth publishing; the only question is how to present them so a peer reviewer — who has not met the author and will read quickly — cannot find a foothold for doubt. This chapter pulls the seven critique chapters into one sequenced plan, then gathers the questions a reviewer is most likely to ask.

8.1 The minimum that lets the central conclusions stand

The headline claims do not require new data. They require tighter grounding in what the reported numbers actually measure. None of what follows requires collecting a single new rating or rebuilding a study from scratch — every fix is a targeted addition or a reframing: better diagnostics where the estimation went unchecked, cleaner operationalizations where the manipulations drifted from their definitions, and modest housekeeping where the reporting left questions open. The architecture of both simulation studies (the dissertation’s Chapters 3 and 4) is sound; tighten the scaffolding and the same intellectual argument becomes airtight.

8.2 A sequenced plan

(“Ch.” below refers to the chapters of this companion, not the dissertation; the order follows logical dependency rather than chapter number.)

Work in this order, because each tier clears the ground for the next. Tier 1 comes first because if “sensitivity” is measuring the wrong thing (a) or the HRM estimates are unstable (b), none of the other numbers can be interpreted at all. Tier 2 then fixes what the manipulations actually estimate, and Tier 3 is genuine housekeeping — real, but not blocking.

8.2.1 Tier 1 — Reframe and re-estimate (highest leverage)

  1. (a) Re-operationalize “sensitivity” as detection. For every condition, report TPR, FPR, and ROC-AUC at the cut score (and parameter recovery), with both normal and effect raters in the analysis so the false-positive rate is visible; retire or explicitly justify the η² ≥ .01 bar. This single move reframes both studies and answers the most fundamental objection (Ch. 5). Effort: medium — one analysis pass, no new simulation.
  2. (b) Re-estimate the HRM with adequate MCMC. Run ≥ 4 independent chains with a longer warm-up; report R-hat, bulk/tail ESS, and MCSE for φ and ψ in every condition; do a prior-sensitivity check with a log-scale prior for ψ; and flag the misfit ψ as non-estimable rather than reporting a sampler-generated value (Ch. 1 + Ch. 4). Effort: higher — re-running estimation is the bottleneck; the model itself does not change.
  3. (c) Diagnose the severe-location collapse. Show parameter recovery (bias, RMSE, coverage) for the severe-rater location against its generating value condition by condition; audit the centering constraint; and disentangle the sample-size confound (N, rater count, and effect-rater count move together). If a one-line centering fix corrects it, document that transparently (Ch. 2). Effort: low–medium.

8.2.2 Tier 2 — Re-operationalize the manipulations

  1. (d) Replace α = 0 “misfit” with a realistic range. A zero slope is a uniform random rater, not a noisy human; use a slope gradient (e.g., α ∈ {0.8, 0.5, 0.2}) plus a halo or central-tendency mechanism, and add a neutral cross-generative cell so neither model is graded on home turf (Ch. 3). Effort: higher — new simulation cells, same framework.
  2. (e) Re-manipulate local dependence on the residual scale. Compute the realized residual correlation per condition; the marginal LI baseline depends on the unit (≈ 0.79 per total score, ≈ 0.43 per item), so 0.2 injects negative residual dependence while 0.6 is a genuine positive violation under the “among items” reading (0.9 on either) — re-manipulate on the residual scale (a shared rater-pair random effect or copula) and re-scope the “MFRM is robust” claim to the conditions that are genuine violations (Ch. 6). Effort: medium.

8.2.3 Tier 3 — Housekeeping

  1. (f) Reconcile the condition count and release the log. Resolve whether the grid is 3⁴ = 81 or 4 × 3³ = 108, trace the stray n = 900, and make the condition log / simulation code available as supplementary material (Ch. 7). Effort: low.
  2. (g) Analyze at the replicate level. Replace per-indicator ANOVAs that ignore clustering with replicate-level summaries or a mixed-effects model, and either justify η² ≥ .01 against a detection baseline or replace it (Ch. 7). Effort: low–medium.
  3. (h) Double-code ~20% of the systematic-review articles. Have a second coder apply the scheme independently and report Cohen’s κ (or Krippendorff’s α) — additive, but expected (Ch. 7). Effort: low.

8.3 The questions to expect, gathered

Chapter The sharpest question a reviewer will ask
1 — ψ identifiability “In what sense is a reported ψ of 16–32 an estimate, given the profile likelihood is flat for ψ ≳ 5 — and why average a non-estimable quantity into an ANOVA?”
2 — Location collapse “Why does the severe-rater location fall monotonically with N when more data should stabilize it? Show recovery vs. true values, condition by condition.”
3 — Misfit operationalization “Is a slope of 0 (uniform noise) a valid model of inaccuracy, and how do you separate the MFRM’s generate-equals-fit advantage from a real finding?”
4 — MCMC adequacy “What are the ESS and MCSE of φ and ψ at 700 draws from one chain — are the interpreted differences actually larger than the MCSE? How was R-hat computed from one chain?”
5 — Sensitivity definition “Your definition is detection — why report η² of means on effect raters only, not TPR/FPR/ROC-AUC at your cut? And why is η² ≥ .01 ‘practical’ when it is near-chance?”
6 — Dependence scale “The marginal LI baseline depends on the unit (≈ 0.79 per total score, ≈ 0.43 per item); under the ‘among items’ reading, 0.2 is below it (not a violation) while 0.6 and 0.9 are genuine violations. Which scale were the targets on — and can a null be read as robustness? Report the residual correlation per condition.”
7 — Housekeeping “Are there 81 or 108 conditions — can you supply the full grid? And what is the inter-coder reliability for the systematic-review coding?”

8.4 Why this is worth doing

Each item on this list is a methodological strength waiting to be unlocked. Reviewers do not reward authors who anticipated no criticism; they reward authors who have already thought the critique through and answered it in the manuscript. Reporting ROC-AUC beside η² is not a concession of weakness — it demonstrates command of the detection-theoretic foundations of the construct. Running four chains and reporting R-hat is not an apology for the dissertation — it demonstrates what adequate MCMC looks like. Documenting the centering audit turns a potential confound into a finding in its own right. The revised studies stop being merely plausible — where a reader must trust the setup — and become persuasive, where a skeptical reader can check the work and find it standing.

The hardest part is already done: two original simulation studies were designed and run, a systematic review completed, and a full dissertation written. The revisions ahead are, by comparison, targeted and finite — and every simulation study in the top measurement journals went through exactly this kind of sharpening. The architecture of the work is sound, and the central conclusions are reachable from it.
