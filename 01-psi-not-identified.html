1  The HRM’s ψ cannot be estimated for misfit raters

  • Show All Code

The Hierarchical Rater Model (HRM) analysis is one of the most technically ambitious parts of the dissertation, and the core idea behind it is sound. This chapter examines a single parameter, the rater-variability parameter ψ, because a subtle estimation problem is hiding in the two “misfit” raters. A journal reviewer is likely to find it, and the question is better answered before it is asked.

Where this is in the dissertation. The signal-detection model is Equation (6), p. 53; the misfit-rater ψ values appear in Tables 16–17, p. 65; the prior on ψ is on p. 61.

1.1 What the dissertation does

The HRM works in two stages. Stage 1 turns a student’s latent ability into an ideal rating ξ — the integer category (0–4) the response really deserves. Stage 2 is a signal-detection model (SDM): given that ideal rating, it describes the category a particular rater actually assigns. Equation (6), p. 53, states it as

\[ P(X = k \mid \xi) \;\propto\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{\bigl(k - (\xi + \phi_r)\bigr)^2}{2\,\psi_r^2}\right), \]

a discretized Gaussian centered on the rater’s ideal-plus-bias score \(\xi + \phi_r\), with spread \(\psi_r\). A small \(\psi_r\) means the rater’s ratings cluster tightly around the ideal; the rater is consistent. As the dissertation puts it, “psi (\(\psi_r\)) represents a rater’s variability, which indicates how consistent the rater is … smaller values are interpreted as higher-quality ratings” (p. 53) — “the rater variability parameter (psi)” (p. 61). That is an entirely sensible reading, for raters who are actually reading the work in front of them.

The dissertation then flags two raters whose ψ is far larger than everyone else’s — about 16.65 and 31.85 (SD 13.31) in Tables 16–17 (p. 65) — and reads these large values as the HRM being sensitive to rater misfit. The intuition is sound. The trouble is what the likelihood surface actually looks like for a rater whose ratings carry no signal.

1.2 Why this threatens the conclusion

It helps to sit with the intuition before the formalism. Imagine a rater who is not reading the essays at all — someone assigning scores by rolling a five-sided die. Their ratings are pure noise, equally likely to land on any category, with no relation to ξ. Now ask what Equation (6) does for that rater. As \(\psi_r\) grows, the Gaussian in the exponent flattens; by \(\psi_r \approx 5\) the five category probabilities are already nearly equal, and as \(\psi_r\) keeps growing the distribution becomes exactly uniform — every category at 0.20, for any ideal rating. In the model’s own language, a rater who chooses at random is a rater with \(\psi_r \to \infty\). Panel A of Figure 1.1 shows this collapse.

That is the heart of the problem. Identification means the data can, in principle, pin down a parameter — that there is a unique value that fits best. The tool for finding it is the profile log-likelihood: the log-likelihood as a function of \(\psi_r\), maximized over everything else. For a well-behaved parameter this curve rises to a clear peak at the best-fitting value (the maximum-likelihood estimate, MLE), then falls away. (Picture a topographic map: we are hunting for the single hilltop — a well-identified parameter has one; a non-identified one is an endless flat ridge with no summit.) For a genuine rater, a stack of essays gives the model real leverage, and the curve peaks cleanly.

For a misfit rater whose ratings are uniform noise, the model cannot tell \(\psi_r = 16\) from \(\psi_r = 32\) from \(\psi_r = 1{,}000\): all of them produce the same near-uniform distribution, so all of them fit equally well. The profile log-likelihood does not peak — it rises and then flattens into a plateau, never turning over into a peak. There is no finite MLE. Panel B of Figure 1.1 shows exactly this. These notes implement Equation (6) from scratch in R — not a re-run of immer, but an independent coding of the dissertation’s own model, done as a diligence check — and plot the profile log-likelihood for two raters. A consistent rater (true \(\psi \approx 0.5\)) shows a sharp peak. A noise rater shows a flat plateau with no peak anywhere out to ψ = 60. The shaded band marks the reported range \(\psi \in [16, 32]\) — and it sits squarely on the flat part of the noise rater’s curve.

So \(\psi = 16\) and \(\psi = 32\) are not two different variability estimates; they are two places the sampler happened to pause while wandering across a flat ridge. The number carries essentially no information about the rater. One might hope the prior would rescue things — but the prior is \(\psi_r \sim N(0.4, 10^2)\) (p. 61), with a standard deviation of 10. This prior is weak, and over small \(\psi\) it is indeed fairly flat — at \(\psi = 5\) its density is still about 90% of the peak at \(\psi = 0.4\), and at \(\psi = 10\) about 63%. But over the reported range it is not flat at all: relative to that peak, the density falls to roughly 30% at \(\psi = 16\) and to about 0.7% at \(\psi = 32\), all but vanishing by \(\psi = 60\). So the prior is not propping these values up — if anything it leans the other way, pulling toward small \(\psi\) and penalizing exactly the large values that were reported. That makes a posterior still settling at \(\psi = 16\)–\(32\) more telling, not less: those numbers survive against the prior’s gentle downward pull, which means it is the unconstrained likelihood ridge — not any help from the prior — that places them out there. (It is also worth noting that a normal prior on a quantity that must be positive is itself a little awkward.) And a plain sanity check seals it: \(\psi = 30\) sets the Gaussian spread parameter in Equation (6) to 30 — seven times the width of the whole 0–4 scale — so the model is essentially uniform whatever the ideal rating. At that point ψ has stopped measuring “consistency” at all: 16 and 30 are not two amounts of variability, just two points on the same flat likelihood ridge.

This matters downstream. Tables 16–17 average ψ across raters and feed it into an ANOVA. Averaging a non-identified quantity yields a mean that inherits the same indeterminacy — so the ANOVA’s “sensitivity” result for ψ rests on numbers that do not mean what the label says.

NoteThe strongest counterargument

The large ψ for those two raters is not a malfunction — it is the HRM working exactly as intended. A rater whose ratings carry no signal genuinely does have unbounded variability, so a ψ that runs out to 16, 32, and beyond is the model faithfully reporting that fact; flagging such raters as extreme is precisely what a sensitivity analysis is for. The dissertation did not paper over the instability — it noted the related \(a_i\) convergence caveat.

And yet — that is the right intuition pointed at the wrong number, and the flag is the part worth keeping. The narrow thing the revision asks is bookkeeping: a quantity the profile likelihood cannot bound (Panel B of Figure 1.1) should be reported as flagged and non-estimable, not entered as a point estimate carrying an SD of 13.31 and then averaged into an ANOVA as though it were measured. “The HRM detected that this rater’s variability is unbounded” and “this rater’s ψ is 16.65” are different claims; only the first is one the data support. Reporting ψ as non-identified is not a retreat from the result — it is the result, in the one form a reviewer cannot dispute.

1.3 What would fix it

The good news: none of this requires new data or abandoning the HRM. It asks for a more careful accounting of what the model can and cannot say about those two raters — and, reframed, the misfit finding actually becomes more interesting.

TipFix-it
  • Treat the misfit ψ as non-estimable. For a rater whose ratings are consistent with uniform noise (Chapter 3 gives a formal criterion), report ψ as non-identified rather than as a point estimate with an SD. This is a substantive finding, not a weakness: the HRM has detected that the rater’s variability is unbounded given the data.
  • Reparameterize on the log scale with a regularizing prior. Work with \(\log\psi_r\) — it keeps ψ positive and tames the tail. Something like \(\log\psi_r \sim N(0, 1)\) (so ψ is a priori roughly 0.1–10) regularizes meaningfully without being dogmatic, and barely moves the well-identified raters.
  • Report diagnostics that separate two questions: is the sampler exploring well (mixing), and can the data pin ψ down at all (identification)? They are not the same, and they are easy to conflate. For mixing, give each rater’s ψ an effective sample size (ESS) and Monte-Carlo standard error (MCSE) — but read them for what they measure: ESS is roughly the retained draws over the autocorrelation time (\(\text{ESS} \approx N/\tau\)), so it simply grows as the chain runs longer and says nothing, by itself, about whether ψ is identified. For identification, lean on the checks this chapter already uses: the profile log-likelihood (a clean peak means the data fix a best ψ; a flat ridge with no finite maximum — Panel B of Figure 1.1 — means they cannot), whether the posterior is essentially just the prior reshaped, posterior mass that drifts toward ever-larger ψ instead of concentrating, and a prior-sensitivity refit (a tighter and a looser prior — a posterior that moves when only the prior changes is prior-driven, not data-driven). The two genuinely come apart: a sampler can glide along the flat ψ ridge with perfectly healthy ESS and still be unable to resolve ψ — which is exactly why the likelihood-side checks are the ones that settle it.
  • Add a posterior-predictive check for the misfit raters — and read its non-failure as the signal. Draw replicated rating sets from the fitted HRM posterior for those two raters and overlay their category frequencies on the observed ratings (a bar-style PPC, or a Bayesian p-value on the category counts). The point is the reverse of a usual check: the model will pass, and pass for every ψ across the reported 16–32 range and well beyond, because once ψ is large the SDM reproduces the near-uniform observed pattern no matter its exact value. A check the data cannot fail — flat across a wide ψ band — is the in-sample, posterior-side counterpart of the flat profile likelihood in Panel B of Figure 1.1: it confirms the ratings simply do not distinguish one large ψ from another.
  • Report a bounded, estimable quantity for the misfit raters in place of an averaged ψ. The mean of a non-identified ψ is not worth tabulating; a classification result is. Give the rate at which the HRM correctly flags the misfit raters as misfitting — a detection/flag rate with its uncertainty (a Wilson or posterior interval over replications) — and/or, per rater, the posterior probability that ψ exceeds a stated “noise” threshold, \(\Pr(\psi_r > \psi^*)\) for a pre-specified cut (Chapter 3’s criterion gives a principled one). These are estimable precisely where the mean of ψ is not: \(\Pr(\psi_r > \psi^*)\) stays near 1 whether the true value is 16 or 1,000, so it is stable along the very ridge that makes the point estimate meaningless. This is the concrete, reportable form of “the HRM flags these raters, it does not measure them.”
  • Limit ψ claims to identified conditions. Restrict the ANOVA and any “sensitivity” language to raters whose ψ has a finite peak; discuss the misfit raters as cases the HRM flags, not cases it measures.

1.4 What a reviewer will ask

WarningWhat a reviewer will ask

“In what sense is a reported ψ of 16–32 an estimate, given that the profile likelihood for these raters has no finite maximum? And what justifies averaging it across raters and entering it into an ANOVA?”

This question is precise, and it is answerable — but only after the revisions above. Done well, the fix turns a vulnerability into a demonstration of methodological care: the diagnostics are run, ψ is found to be non-identified for those two raters, and it is reported accordingly. That is what careful Bayesian practice looks like, and it makes the misfit result stronger, not weaker — the HRM correctly flags raters whose variability the data cannot bound.

reported. That makes a posterior still settling at $\psi = 16$–$32$ *more* telling, not
less: those numbers survive *against* the prior's gentle downward pull, which means it is
the unconstrained likelihood ridge — not any help from the prior — that places them out
there. (It is also worth noting that a
*normal* prior on a quantity that must be positive is itself a little awkward.) And a
plain sanity check seals it: $\psi = 30$ sets the Gaussian *spread parameter* in
Equation (6) to 30 — seven times the width of the whole 0–4 scale — so the model is
essentially uniform whatever the ideal rating. At that point ψ has stopped measuring
"consistency" at all: 16 and 30 are not two amounts of variability, just two points on
the same flat likelihood ridge.

This matters downstream. Tables 16–17 average ψ across raters and feed it into an
ANOVA. Averaging a non-identified quantity yields a mean that inherits the same
indeterminacy — so the ANOVA's "sensitivity" result for ψ rests on numbers that do not
mean what the label says.

::: {.callout-note}
## The strongest counterargument

The large ψ for those two raters is not a malfunction — it is the HRM
working *exactly as intended*. A rater whose ratings carry no signal genuinely *does* have
unbounded variability, so a ψ that runs out to 16, 32, and beyond is the model faithfully
reporting that fact; flagging such raters as extreme is precisely what a sensitivity
analysis is *for*. The dissertation did not paper over the instability — it noted the related $a_i$
convergence caveat.

And yet — that is the right intuition pointed at the wrong number, and the flag is the part
worth keeping. The narrow thing the revision asks is bookkeeping: a quantity the profile
likelihood cannot bound (Panel B of @fig-psi-identified) should be *reported as flagged and
non-estimable*, not entered as a point estimate carrying an SD of 13.31 and then *averaged
into an ANOVA* as though it were measured. "The HRM detected that this rater's variability is
unbounded" and "this rater's ψ is 16.65" are different claims; only the first is one the data
support. Reporting ψ as non-identified is not a retreat from the result — it *is* the
result, in the one form a reviewer cannot dispute.
:::

## What would fix it

The good news: none of this requires new data or abandoning the HRM. It asks for a more
careful accounting of what the model can and cannot say about those two raters — and,
reframed, the misfit finding actually becomes *more* interesting.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Fix-it
- **Treat the misfit ψ as non-estimable.** For a rater whose ratings are consistent
  with uniform noise (Chapter 3 gives a formal criterion), report ψ as non-identified
  rather than as a point estimate with an SD. This is a substantive finding, not a
  weakness: the HRM has detected that the rater's variability is *unbounded* given the
  data.
- **Reparameterize on the log scale with a regularizing prior.** Work with
  $\log\psi_r$ — it keeps ψ positive and tames the tail. Something like
  $\log\psi_r \sim N(0, 1)$ (so ψ is a priori roughly 0.1–10) regularizes meaningfully
  without being dogmatic, and barely moves the well-identified raters.
- **Report diagnostics that separate two questions: is the sampler exploring well
  (*mixing*), and can the data pin ψ down at all (*identification*)?** They are not the
  same, and they are easy to conflate. For *mixing*, give each rater's ψ an effective
  sample size (ESS) and Monte-Carlo standard error (MCSE) — but read them for what they
  measure: ESS is roughly the retained draws over the autocorrelation time
  ($\text{ESS} \approx N/\tau$), so it simply *grows* as the chain runs longer and says
  nothing, by itself, about whether ψ is identified. For *identification*, lean on the
  checks this chapter already uses: the **profile log-likelihood** (a clean peak means the
  data fix a best ψ; a flat ridge with no finite maximum — Panel B of @fig-psi-identified
  — means they cannot), whether the **posterior is essentially just the prior reshaped**,
  posterior mass that **drifts toward ever-larger ψ instead of concentrating**, and a
  **prior-sensitivity refit** (a tighter and a looser prior — a posterior that moves when
  only the prior changes is prior-driven, not data-driven). The two genuinely come apart:
  a sampler can glide along the flat ψ ridge with perfectly healthy ESS and still be
  unable to resolve ψ — which is exactly why the likelihood-side checks are the ones that
  settle it.
- **Add a posterior-predictive check for the misfit raters — and read its *non*-failure as
  the signal.** Draw replicated rating sets from the fitted HRM posterior for those two
  raters and overlay their category frequencies on the observed ratings (a bar-style PPC,
  or a Bayesian *p*-value on the category counts). The point is the reverse of a usual
  check: the model will *pass*, and pass for *every* ψ across the reported 16–32 range and
  well beyond, because once ψ is large the SDM reproduces the near-uniform observed pattern
  no matter its exact value. A check the data cannot fail — flat across a wide ψ band — is
  the in-sample, posterior-side counterpart of the flat profile likelihood in Panel B of
  @fig-psi-identified: it confirms the ratings simply do not distinguish one large ψ from
  another.
- **Report a bounded, estimable quantity for the misfit raters in place of an averaged ψ.**
  The mean of a non-identified ψ is not worth tabulating; a *classification* result is.
  Give the rate at which the HRM correctly flags the misfit raters as misfitting — a
  detection/flag rate with its uncertainty (a Wilson or posterior interval over
  replications) — and/or, per rater, the posterior probability that ψ exceeds a stated
  "noise" threshold, $\Pr(\psi_r > \psi^*)$ for a pre-specified cut (Chapter 3's criterion
  gives a principled one). These are estimable precisely where the mean of ψ is not:
  $\Pr(\psi_r > \psi^*)$ stays near 1 whether the true value is 16 or 1,000, so it is stable
  along the very ridge that makes the point estimate meaningless. This is the concrete,
  reportable form of "the HRM *flags* these raters, it does not *measure* them."
- **Limit ψ claims to identified conditions.** Restrict the ANOVA and any "sensitivity"
  language to raters whose ψ has a finite peak; discuss the misfit raters as cases the
  HRM *flags*, not cases it *measures*.
:::

## What a reviewer will ask

::: {.callout-warning}
## What a reviewer will ask
"In what sense is a reported ψ of 16–32 an *estimate*, given that the profile likelihood
for these raters has no finite maximum? And what justifies averaging it across raters
and entering it into an ANOVA?"
:::

This question is precise, and it is answerable — but only after the revisions above.
Done well, the fix turns a vulnerability into a demonstration of methodological care: the
diagnostics are run, ψ is found to be non-identified for those two raters, and it is reported
accordingly. That is what careful Bayesian practice looks like, and it makes
the misfit result *stronger*, not weaker — the HRM correctly flags raters whose
variability the data cannot bound.
