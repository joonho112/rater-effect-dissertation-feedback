This chapter concerns one of the most puzzling numbers in the dissertation — a result that contradicts what a working estimator should do and that resists explanation from the outside. The thread, once pulled, leads somewhere instructive and would make the journal manuscript markedly stronger. The numbers are in Table 14 (p. 64); the design is on pp. 55–56.

2.1 What the dissertation does

A rater’s location in the Many-Facet Rasch Model (MFRM) is a number on the logit scale that says how much harsher or easier that rater is than the group: a positive location marks a severe rater (one who tends to assign lower scores). In the condition where some raters are generated as genuinely severe — their true generating mean is \(\lambda = 2.0\) — the estimated severe-rater location, in the complete design, reads:

Examinee N Severe-rater location (Table 14, p. 64)
50 1.61
100 0.62
500 0.26

The dissertation reads this as severe raters being most distinctly identified when the sample is small. The design is transparent: rater count scales at one rater per ten examinees (p. 55), so there are 5, 10, and 50 raters at the three sample sizes; 10% of them are severe effect raters (p. 56) — which works out to 1, 1, and 5 effect raters. Several SD cells are blank because some conditions “include only 1 or 2 raters” (p. 64). And the normal raters in this same severe condition come out positive (+0.40, +0.07, +0.03) where a mean-centered scale predicts the opposite sign — and at N = 50 the discrepancy is exact. With one severe rater (\(\lambda = 2\)) among five, mean-centering puts the grand offset at \(2/5 = +0.40\), so a centered scale predicts the severe rater at \(2 - 0.40 = +1.60\) (the reported 1.61, essentially on the nose) and the four normal raters at \(0 - 0.40 = -0.40\). They are instead reported at +0.40 — the predicted magnitude with its sign reversed, not merely a little high; so the five locations average \((1.61 + 4 \times 0.40)/5 \approx +0.64\) rather than the \(\approx 0\) a mean-centered scale requires. That clean reversal — not the overall drift — is the sharp end of the thread, pointing to the centering/sign step (a sign convention, an anchoring choice, or a condition-labeling step) behaving unlike a mean-centered MFRM, to be pinned down by a look at the code. Hold that whole picture in mind: the severe group drifts toward zero as N grows, the normal group also drifts, and at the two smallest N the “group mean” is one rater’s estimate.

2.2 Why this threatens the conclusion

The numbers move in the wrong direction. The true severity is \(\lambda \approx 2.0\). A working estimator, given more data, should pull the estimate toward the truth and hold it there — that is essentially the definition of a consistent estimator. Instead the estimate is closest to the truth at N = 50 (1.61) and has collapsed to 0.26 — about one-seventh of the true value — by N = 500. More information has made the answer worse.

The most likely culprit is the centering (identification) constraint. Because MFRM locations are defined only relative to one another, the software must fix their mean (or sum) to zero before it can report individual values. So a reported “severe” location is not absolute severity; it is severity relative to the average of whatever raters are in that run. At N = 50 there is 1 severe rater among 5; at N = 500 there are 5 among 50. If the non-severe raters’ estimates also drift — and their reported positive sign, where a mean-centered scale predicts a negative one (−0.40 at N = 50), says they do — then the zero point itself is moving from condition to condition, and the reported collapse is partly a measurement of that moving reference point rather than of the raters.

There is also a confound sitting underneath the word “sample size.” Increasing N from 50 to 500 changes four things at once: the precision of each estimate (the Fisher standard error falls from about 0.072 to 0.051 to 0.023), the number of raters (5 → 10 → 50), the number of severe raters (1 → 1 → 5), and the number of ratings each rater gives (250 → 500 → 2,500). At N = 50 and N = 100 the “severe location mean” is therefore one rater’s estimate, and “sample size” is not a clean experimental lever at all.

To see whether the collapse is a property of the model or of the pipeline, an independent marginal-ML MFRM was implemented in R, with the true ability distribution and item parameters held known — this is not a re-run of TAM, but a separate implementation built only to check recovery against ground truth. It recovers a stable severe location of roughly 1.5–2.0 at every sample size (about 1.50, 2.03, and 1.85). It does exactly what more data should do: it holds the estimate steady and tightens the uncertainty. Figure 2.1 puts the two side by side.

source("R/common.R")

set.seed(99)
I <- 5L; Ns <- c(50L, 100L, 500L); delta_true <- rnorm(I, 0, 0.5)

# average model score variance W, and Fisher SE of a rater location
W       <- mean(vapply(seq_len(I), function(i)
             mean(score_variance(pcmfrm_probs(rnorm(40000), 0, delta_true[i], alpha = 1))), numeric(1)))
se_by_N <- 1 / sqrt(Ns * I * W)
n_eff   <- pmax(1L, round(0.1 * (Ns %/% 10L)))          # 1, 1, 5
rpr     <- I * Ns                                        # 250, 500, 2500

# ---- independent marginal-ML recovery (Gauss–Hermite over theta ~ N(0,1)) ----
gh <- statmod::gauss.quad(41L, "hermite"); theta_q <- sqrt(2) * gh$nodes; wq <- gh$weights / sqrt(pi)
lam_grid <- seq(-1, 4, by = 0.05); G <- length(lam_grid)
marg <- array(0, c(G, I, K))
for (g in seq_len(G)) for (i in seq_len(I))
  marg[g, i, ] <- colSums(pcmfrm_probs(theta_q, lam_grid[g], delta_true[i], alpha = 1) * wq)
log_marg <- log(pmax(marg, 1e-300))

estimate_lambda <- function(counts) {                    # grid argmax + parabolic refine
  ll <- vapply(seq_len(G), function(g) sum(counts * log_marg[g, , ]), numeric(1))
  g <- which.max(ll)
  if (g > 1L && g < G) {
    h <- lam_grid[2] - lam_grid[1]; d <- ll[g-1] - 2*ll[g] + ll[g+1]
    if (abs(d) > 1e-12) return(lam_grid[g] + max(-h, min(h, -0.5*h*(ll[g+1]-ll[g-1])/d)))
  }
  lam_grid[g]
}
recover <- function(N, n_rep) {                          # mean centered severe location
  R <- N %/% 10L; ne <- pmax(1L, round(0.1 * R))
  vapply(seq_len(n_rep), function(.) {
    lam <- rnorm(R, 0, 0.5); eff <- sample(R, ne); lam[eff] <- rnorm(ne, 2, 0.5)
    th  <- rnorm(N)
    lam_hat <- vapply(seq_len(R), function(r) {
      counts <- matrix(0L, I, K)
      for (i in seq_len(I)) { x <- simulate_rating(pcmfrm_probs(th, lam[r], delta_true[i], alpha = 1))
        for (k in seq_len(K)) counts[i, k] <- sum(x == (k - 1L)) }
      estimate_lambda(counts)
    }, numeric(1))
    mean((lam_hat - mean(lam_hat))[eff])
  }, numeric(1))
}
n_rep_by_N <- c(50L, 25L, 8L)
rec <- lapply(seq_along(Ns), function(j) { set.seed(100L + j); recover(Ns[j], n_rep_by_N[j]) })
rec_m <- vapply(rec, mean, numeric(1)); rec_s <- vapply(rec, sd, numeric(1))

diss_sev <- c(1.61, 0.62, 0.26); diss_norm <- c(0.40, 0.07, 0.03)
col_rec <- "#117A65"; col_sev <- "#C0392B"; col_norm <- "#4C4C4C"

So the most plausible reading is that the collapse comes from the centering, anchoring, or condition-labeling step — a reference point that shifts with N — rather than from the MFRM. This is not a flaw in the core argument; it is a pipeline detail that, once found, will very likely restore the stable recovery the theory predicts.

2.3 What would fix it

TipFix-it
  • Report recovery as a per-condition table, not just estimates. Build one recovery table with a row per condition × rater type (severe vs. normal) and named columns: true severity \(\lambda\), raw (uncentered) estimate, centered estimate, bias (centered − true), RMSE, coverage, Monte-Carlo SE, and convergence status. Pair it with the true-vs-estimated \(\lambda\) scatterplot faceted by condition, and keep the rank correlation between estimated and true severity across raters. Named columns make the diagnosis answerable at a glance: raw beside centered shows whether the centering step is what moves (the +0.40 sign reversal jumps out there), bias beside RMSE separates a systematic shift from noise, coverage flags intervals that are too narrow, and Monte-Carlo SE with convergence status guards against reading a sampler artifact — especially in the single-effect-rater cells — as a finding.
  • Untangle the confound. Replace the single “sample size” factor with a grid that separates examinee N, rater count, effect-rater count, and ratings per rater. Even a post-hoc table labeling each existing condition by all four clarifies what was actually manipulated.
  • Audit the centering and its sign, condition by condition. Print the sum (and mean) of estimated rater locations per replication. At N = 50 the tell is sharp: a mean-centered solution forces the normal raters to \(-0.40\) and the five-rater mean to \(0\), yet they are reported at \(+0.40\) with the locations averaging \(\approx +0.64\) — the predicted normal value with its sign reversed. Check the sign convention on the centering/anchoring call, not just whether a constraint is applied; the positive normal-rater values (+0.40 / 0.07 / 0.03) are the thread to pull.
  • Add a sensitivity analysis for the single-effect-rater cells (N = 50, 100), where the “severe mean” is one estimate, and flag those cells explicitly.
  • Share the simulation code and condition logs. One look at the call that produces the N = 500 results will most likely make the source of the collapse visible.

2.4 What a reviewer will ask

WarningWhat a reviewer will ask

“Why does the severe-rater location decline from about 1.61 to 0.26 as the sample grows, when more information should stabilize it toward the true value? Please show the recovery of the rater locations against their true generating values, condition by condition, and confirm the centering constraint is applied consistently across sample sizes.”

The number is strange precisely because it contradicts what MFRM estimation should do. Strange numbers, followed carefully, almost always lead somewhere worth knowing: a line in the pipeline, a condition label, a centering call that behaves differently at 5 raters than at 50. Tracking it down would yield a thorough understanding of the internals of this simulation — the footing the journal version requires.

So the most plausible reading is that the collapse comes from the centering, anchoring,
or condition-labeling step — a reference point that shifts with N — rather than from the
MFRM. This is not a flaw in the core argument; it is a pipeline detail that, once
found, will very likely *restore* the stable recovery the theory predicts.

## What would fix it

::: {.callout-tip}
## Fix-it
- **Report recovery as a per-condition table, not just estimates.** Build one recovery
  table with a row per condition × rater type (severe vs. normal) and named columns:
  *true severity* $\lambda$, *raw (uncentered) estimate*, *centered estimate*, *bias*
  (centered − true), *RMSE*, *coverage*, *Monte-Carlo SE*, and *convergence status*. Pair
  it with the true-vs-estimated $\lambda$ scatterplot faceted by condition, and keep the
  rank correlation between estimated and true severity across raters. Named columns make
  the diagnosis answerable at a glance: *raw* beside *centered* shows whether the centering
  step is what moves (the +0.40 sign reversal jumps out there), *bias* beside *RMSE*
  separates a systematic shift from noise, *coverage* flags intervals that are too narrow,
  and *Monte-Carlo SE* with *convergence status* guards against reading a sampler
  artifact — especially in the single-effect-rater cells — as a finding.
- **Untangle the confound.** Replace the single "sample size" factor with a grid that
  separates examinee N, rater count, effect-rater count, and ratings per rater. Even a
  post-hoc table labeling each existing condition by all four clarifies what was
  actually manipulated.
- **Audit the centering *and its sign*, condition by condition.** Print the sum (and mean)
  of estimated rater locations per replication. At N = 50 the tell is sharp: a mean-centered
  solution forces the normal raters to $-0.40$ and the five-rater mean to $0$, yet they are
  reported at $+0.40$ with the locations averaging $\approx +0.64$ — the predicted normal
  value with its sign reversed. Check the sign convention on the centering/anchoring call,
  not just whether a constraint is applied; the positive normal-rater values
  (+0.40 / 0.07 / 0.03) are the thread to pull.
- **Add a sensitivity analysis for the single-effect-rater cells** (N = 50, 100), where
  the "severe mean" is one estimate, and flag those cells explicitly.
- **Share the simulation code and condition logs.** One look at the call that produces
  the N = 500 results will most likely make the source of the collapse visible.
:::

## What a reviewer will ask

::: {.callout-warning}
## What a reviewer will ask
"Why does the severe-rater location decline from about 1.61 to 0.26 as the sample grows,
when more information should stabilize it toward the true value? Please show the recovery
of the rater locations against their true generating values, condition by condition,
and confirm the centering constraint is applied consistently across sample sizes."
:::

The number is strange precisely because it contradicts what MFRM estimation should do.
Strange numbers, followed carefully, almost always lead somewhere worth knowing: a line in
the pipeline, a condition label, a centering call that behaves differently at 5 raters than
at 50. Tracking it down would yield a thorough understanding of the internals of this
simulation — the footing the journal version requires.
