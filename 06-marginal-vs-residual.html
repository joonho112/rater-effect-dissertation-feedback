Chapter 4 takes seriously a threat (rater dependence) that most applied MFRM studies ignore, and builds a simulation to probe it. The revision concerns sharpening the interpretation so the study says precisely what it shows, and no more. The relevant pages are 76–90.

6.1 What the dissertation does

Local independence — in Yen’s (1993) formulation, as quoted on page 76 — is a property of residual correlations: a pair of scores is locally independent when they are “independent after controlling for the latent variable.” In a many-facet Rasch context that means two raters’ scores on the same examinees should be uncorrelated once examinee ability, rater severity, and item difficulty have been accounted for. The residual is whatever shared signal remains after the model has explained what it can.

Chapter 4 asks whether MFRM indices are sensitive to violations of that property. The design on page 84 selects rater pairs so that about 20% of raters belong to a correlated pair, then “replaced one rater’s scores with values that had a specific correlation with their partner’s scores” at three target levels — 0.2, 0.6, and 0.9 — describing this as reflecting “Yen’s (1993) definition of local independence as low correlations among items after controlling for examinee ability” (pp. 84–85). The result, on page 90, is that “the practical impact of rater dependence was minimal across conditions, suggesting that the MFRM is robust to this type of potential local independence violation.”

The logic is clean and the implementation careful. The one problem is that the manipulation operates on a marginal (raw, unconditional) correlation between the two raters’ scores — not on the residual correlation the conclusion invokes. And because that marginal baseline depends on the unit (total score vs. single item), even the sign of the dependence some conditions impose turns out to be ambiguous.

6.2 Why this threatens the conclusion

Start from first principles. Imagine two raters, A and B, scoring the same pool of examinees independently — no consultation, no shared bias. Under perfect local independence their residual correlation is zero by definition. Yet both are doing the same job: each tracks examinee ability. When ability is high, both tend to award higher scores; when it is low, both score lower. That shared pull from the latent variable creates a positive marginal correlation between A and B even when they are perfectly locally independent.

Think of two thermometers hanging in the same room. Neither influences the other, but both respond to the room temperature, so their readings move together. The correlation is not evidence of dependence between the thermometers — it is evidence of the shared environment they both measure. Rater A and rater B are two thermometers; examinee ability is the room.

The magnitude was verified by simulating two locally-independent partial-credit MFRM raters scoring the same examinees in R — not a re-run of TAM, but a ground-up construction. Under perfect local independence the marginal correlation of their total scores was about 0.79 — and of their ratings on a single shared item, about 0.43 — while a residual correlation after partialling out ability was about 0.03, indistinguishable from zero. That ≈ 0.03 is a simplified illustration of the residual idea — the partial correlation of the two raters’ total scores given ability — rather than Yen’s Q3 itself: Yen’s (1993) Q3 proper correlates the item-level residuals from the fitted model (observed minus model-expected for each person × item) across item or rater pairs. The illustration still makes the point — under local independence the residual sits near zero — but a real check would report Q3. Both marginal numbers matter; the targets land differently against each. Figure 6.1 makes the geometry visible.

partial_corr <- function(x, y, z) {                  # corr(x, y | z), z quadratic
  Z <- cbind(1, z, z^2); cor(lm.fit(Z, x)$residuals, lm.fit(Z, y)$residuals)
}
two_rater_totals <- function(n) {                    # two LI raters, same examinees

The targets must be read against that baseline — but first which baseline must be decided, because it depends on the unit correlated. Two raters’ 5-item total scores are marginally correlated at ≈ 0.79 under perfect local independence; their ratings on the same single item are correlated at only ≈ 0.43, because the shared-ability signal accumulates across five items while a lone ordinal rating carries far less of it. This distinction is decisive, because the dissertation fixes the unit at the item level: page 84 describes “replac[ing] one rater’s scores,” and pages 84–85 ground the design in “Yen’s (1993) definition of local independence as low correlations among items.” Among items — so the baseline that matters is the per-item one (≈ 0.43), not the total-score one (≈ 0.79).

Read against both baselines, the three targets split three ways rather than two (Panel A):

  • 0.2 sits below both baselines. Reaching it requires injecting negative residual correlation — suppressing the natural ability-driven co-movement, as if the raters were anti-coordinated after controlling for ability. On either unit, this is the opposite of a positive violation.
  • 0.9 sits above both baselines — a genuine positive violation on either reading.
  • 0.6 sits between the two: below the total-score baseline of 0.79, but above the item-level baseline of 0.43. Its verdict therefore depends on the scale the manipulation operated on. Read at the total-score level it would still imply mild negative residual dependence; but read at the item/rating level — the reading the dissertation’s own “among items” wording implies — 0.6 is a genuine positive violation, and a near-null MFRM response would mean the model absorbed a real violation rather than that none was there.

That cuts partly in the dissertation’s favour. On the more defensible (item-level) reading, the 0.6 and 0.9 conditions did impose genuine positive dependence, so a null result is not merely an artefact of removing co-movement. What the design still cannot settle is which scale was in force — the manipulation’s unit was never stated, and the residual correlation that actually entered the MFRM was never measured. So a null result remains consistent with several stories (the MFRM is genuinely robust; the 0.2 condition imposed negative dependence and could never have flagged; or the raw-score replacement created an item-level structure the model quietly absorbed), and the data as they stand cannot tell them apart.

There is a quieter issue too: overwriting one rater’s scores with a constructed vector changes not just the pair correlation but that rater’s score distribution and severity estimate — so the residual correlation that actually entered the MFRM (the quantity Yen’s definition is about) was never directly measured.

NoteThe strongest counterargument

In the dissertation’s defense, the manipulation follows Yen’s (1993) “low correlations among items” framing directly — it is grounded in his definition and operates at the item level, the unit his wording names. It does genuinely move the rater-pair correlation; the conditions are not inert. And a near-null MFRM response is not nothing — it is positive, informative evidence about the model’s robustness to the induced dependence.

And yet — granted, and this cuts partly in the dissertation’s favour: under the item-level reading, the 0.6 and 0.9 conditions sit above the ≈ 0.43 baseline (Panel A) and so are genuine positive violations the MFRM apparently absorbed, which is a point in its favour, not against it. What the design cannot yet settle is the cleanness of the reading, for two stated reasons — the manipulation’s scale was never declared, and the realized residual correlation that actually entered the MFRM was never measured. Until both are reported (ideally as Yen’s Q3 per condition), a flat result stays equally consistent with “the MFRM is robust” and with “the 0.2 condition imposed negative dependence that could never flag.” Reporting the realized residual per condition is the one addition that turns an ambiguous null into a measured dose — and on the item-level reading, it likely settles the matter in the dissertation’s favour.

6.3 What would fix it

TipFix-it
  • Report realized correlations at both scales. Per condition, give (a) the realized marginal correlation — at both the total-score and the item/rating level — and (b) the realized residual correlation, also at both levels: Yen’s Q3 — the correlation of item-level residuals from the fitted MFRM (observed minus model-expected per person × item) — which is the proper diagnostic, shown beside the total-score partial correlation given ability (the ≈ 0.03 illustration above) for comparison. Q3 is the quantity that settles each condition; the total-score partial is reported only so a reader can see the two readings agree. This one addition lets a reviewer see where each condition truly sits.
  • Re-manipulate on the residual scale. Induce dependence directly in residual space — a shared rater-pair random effect added to the log-odds (cleanest), a residual copula, a testlet-style MFRM pair effect, or a training-group random effect — sweeping a known residual correlation across, say, −0.3 to +0.5 around the LI baseline of 0.
  • Separate two phenomena. Repeated-ratings dependence (the same script scored twice) and contextual dependence (a shared training cohort) have different residual structures; distinguishing them sharpens the contribution.
  • Compare with dependence-aware models. Ask not only whether MFRM flags the dependence but whether a rater-bundle model, random-effect facet model, testlet MFRM, or HRM recovers parameters better when genuine positive residual dependence is present.
  • Re-scope the conclusion. State it as “MFRM indices changed little under this specific score-replacement manipulation” — noting that on the item-level reading the 0.6 and 0.9 conditions plausibly did impose positive dependence the model absorbed — not “robust to local-independence violations” in general.

6.4 What a reviewer will ask

WarningWhat a reviewer will ask

“Could you report the realized residual correlation given ability in each condition, and state the unit your target correlations were defined on? Two raters scoring the same examinees are already marginally correlated under perfect local independence — at ≈ 0.79 on their total scores, but only ≈ 0.43 on a single shared item. Since you cite Yen’s ‘low correlations among items,’ the item-level baseline of ≈ 0.43 looks like the relevant one — and on that reading, 0.2 sits below it (negative residual dependence, not a violation) while 0.6 and 0.9 sit above it and are genuine positive violations. Could you confirm which scale the manipulation operated on, and report the realized residual correlation per condition, so a null result can be read as ‘the MFRM absorbed real positive dependence’ rather than left ambiguous?”

Whether the MFRM holds up when raters are not independent is a question the field needs answered, and this study builds the machinery to answer it. The fix is small relative to the idea — move the manipulation onto the residual scale, report the residual correlation actually induced, and let the conclusion follow. Done that way, this becomes a stronger paper than a clean null would have been: a study that pins down precisely how much residual dependence the MFRM can absorb, with the dose measured on the scale Yen actually defined.

The targets must be read against that baseline — but first *which* baseline must be decided, because it
depends on the unit correlated. Two raters' **5-item total scores** are marginally
correlated at ≈ **0.79** under perfect local independence; their ratings on the **same single
item** are correlated at only ≈ **0.43**, because the shared-ability signal accumulates across
five items while a lone ordinal rating carries far less of it. This distinction is decisive,
because the dissertation fixes the unit at the item level: page 84 describes "replac[ing] one
rater's scores," and pages 84–85 ground the design in "Yen's (1993) definition of local
independence as low correlations *among items*." *Among items* — so the baseline that matters
is the per-item one (≈ 0.43), not the total-score one (≈ 0.79).

Read against both baselines, the three targets split three ways rather than two (Panel A):

- **0.2** sits below *both* baselines. Reaching it requires injecting *negative* residual
  correlation — suppressing the natural ability-driven co-movement, as if the raters were
  *anti*-coordinated after controlling for ability. On either unit, this is the opposite of a
  positive violation.
- **0.9** sits above *both* baselines — a genuine positive violation on either reading.
- **0.6** sits *between* the two: below the total-score baseline of 0.79, but above the
  item-level baseline of 0.43. Its verdict therefore depends on the scale the manipulation
  operated on. Read at the total-score level it would still imply mild *negative* residual
  dependence; but read at the item/rating level — the reading the dissertation's own "among
  items" wording implies — **0.6 is a genuine positive violation**, and a near-null MFRM
  response would mean the model *absorbed* a real violation rather than that none was there.

That cuts partly in the dissertation's favour. On the more defensible (item-level) reading, the 0.6 and 0.9
conditions did impose genuine positive dependence, so a null result is not *merely* an
artefact of removing co-movement. What the design still cannot settle is which scale was in
force — the manipulation's unit was never stated, and the residual correlation that actually
entered the MFRM was never measured. So a null result remains consistent with several stories
(the MFRM is genuinely robust; the 0.2 condition imposed negative dependence and could never
have flagged; or the raw-score replacement created an item-level structure the model quietly
absorbed), and the data as they stand cannot tell them apart.

There is a quieter issue too: overwriting one rater's scores with a constructed vector
changes not just the pair correlation but that rater's score distribution and severity
estimate — so the residual correlation that actually entered the MFRM (the quantity Yen's
definition is about) was never directly measured.

::: {.callout-note}
## The strongest counterargument

In the dissertation's defense, the manipulation follows Yen's (1993) "low correlations *among items*"
framing directly — it is grounded in his definition and operates at the item level, the
unit his wording names. It does genuinely move the rater-pair correlation; the conditions
are not inert. And a near-null MFRM response is not nothing — it is positive, informative
evidence about the model's robustness to the induced dependence.

And yet — granted, and this cuts partly in the dissertation's favour: under the item-level reading, the
0.6 and 0.9 conditions sit *above* the ≈ 0.43 baseline (Panel A) and so are genuine positive
violations the MFRM apparently *absorbed*, which is a point in its favour, not against it.
What the design cannot yet settle is the *cleanness* of the reading, for two stated reasons —
the manipulation's scale was never declared, and the realized residual correlation that
actually entered the MFRM was never measured. Until both are reported (ideally as Yen's Q3
per condition), a flat result stays equally consistent with "the MFRM is robust" and with
"the 0.2 condition imposed *negative* dependence that could never flag." Reporting the
realized residual per condition is the one addition that turns an ambiguous null into a
measured dose — and on the item-level reading, it likely settles the matter in the dissertation's favour.
:::

## What would fix it

::: {.callout-tip}
## Fix-it
- **Report realized correlations at both scales.** Per condition, give (a) the realized
  marginal correlation — at both the total-score and the item/rating level — and (b) the
  realized *residual* correlation, also at both levels: Yen's **Q3** — the correlation of
  *item-level residuals from the fitted MFRM* (observed minus model-expected per person ×
  item) — which is the proper diagnostic, shown beside the total-score partial correlation
  given ability (the ≈ 0.03 illustration above) for comparison. Q3 is the quantity that
  settles each condition; the total-score partial is reported only so a reader can see the
  two readings agree. This one addition lets a reviewer see where each condition truly sits.
- **Re-manipulate on the residual scale.** Induce dependence directly in residual space — a
  shared rater-pair random effect added to the log-odds (cleanest), a residual copula, a
  testlet-style MFRM pair effect, or a training-group random effect — sweeping a known
  residual correlation across, say, −0.3 to +0.5 around the LI baseline of 0.
- **Separate two phenomena.** Repeated-ratings dependence (the same script scored twice) and
  contextual dependence (a shared training cohort) have different residual structures;
  distinguishing them sharpens the contribution.
- **Compare with dependence-aware models.** Ask not only whether MFRM flags the dependence
  but whether a rater-bundle model, random-effect facet model, testlet MFRM, or HRM recovers
  parameters better when genuine positive residual dependence is present.
- **Re-scope the conclusion.** State it as "MFRM indices changed little under this specific
  score-replacement manipulation" — noting that on the item-level reading the 0.6 and 0.9
  conditions plausibly *did* impose positive dependence the model absorbed — not "robust to
  local-independence violations" in general.
:::

## What a reviewer will ask

::: {.callout-warning}
## What a reviewer will ask
"Could you report the realized residual correlation given ability in each condition, and state
the unit your target correlations were defined on? Two raters scoring the same examinees are
already marginally correlated under perfect local independence — at ≈ 0.79 on their total
scores, but only ≈ 0.43 on a single shared item. Since you cite Yen's 'low correlations *among
items*,' the item-level baseline of ≈ 0.43 looks like the relevant one — and on that reading,
0.2 sits below it (negative residual dependence, not a violation) while 0.6 and 0.9 sit *above*
it and are genuine positive violations. Could you confirm which scale the manipulation operated
on, and report the realized residual correlation per condition, so a null result can be read as
'the MFRM absorbed real positive dependence' rather than left ambiguous?"
:::

Whether the MFRM holds up when raters are *not* independent is a question the field needs
answered, and this study builds the machinery to answer it. The fix is small relative to the idea —
move the manipulation onto the residual scale, report the residual correlation actually
induced, and let the conclusion follow. Done that way, this becomes a *stronger* paper than
a clean null would have been: a study that pins down precisely how much residual dependence
the MFRM can absorb, with the dose measured on the scale Yen actually defined.
